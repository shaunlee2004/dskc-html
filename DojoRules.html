Rules and Regulations for Students

These are rules and regulations for the Dominion Shotokan Karate Club, LLC (DSKC). They have been designed to help students feel more
comfortable from the beginning and instill a basic knowledge of the culture, terminology and procedures that are required in a dojo. It will require
some time and effort during your training to learn the proper procedures, rules of etiquette and aspects of Japanese culture. This is understood as
practicing karate is much different than most endeavors you have undertaken in your life thus far. You will make mistakes; learn from them. If you
have questions, feel free to ask the sensei or a sempai at the proper time. We are all here to help you in your journey.

We practice the Japanese art of Shotokan Karate and are affiliated with the International Shotokan Karate Federation (ISKF) headquartered in
Philadelphia, Pennsylvania. The chairman and chief instructor is Master Teruyuki Okazaki, 10th Dan and a direct student of the founder of
Shotokan, Master Gichin Funakoshi. All students are required to become members of the ISKF and can participate in all ISKF tournaments,
special trainings, Master Camps and Fall Camps. In addition, students who are traveling should be able to practice at any ISKF dojo, worldwide,
for a nominal fee. You will need to see your instructor prior to your trip to obtain a letter of introduction for the sensei at the other dojo, to discuss the
small gift, omiyage, you should bring to the sensei and about your expected conduct. Remember, you are representing our dojo; follow all their
rules and do your best during your training visit.

“The ultimate aim of karate-do lies not in victory or defeat, but in the perfection of character of the person who studies it.”
Master Gichin Funakoshi

Frequently Asked Questions

What is Karate?

Karate is a method of unarmed self-defense which was developed on the island of Okinawa. The term Kara-te literally means “empty hand.” The
benefits of karate training extend far beyond self-defense; it is also a method for personal development as well. Karate practitioners develop
character through training and learn a great deal about themselves as a person. Karate is a lifelong process.

Who Can Learn Karate?

Karate is for everyone, regardless of age or physical condition. Conditioning and stamina will improve through training. The benefits of training are
cumulative so the beginning student should be prepared to practice a minimum of two or three days per week. Advanced students can practice
more, however should be careful to avoid overtraining. Karate training is a lifelong endeavor; it cannot be learned in one day. Pace yourself.

What Will I Need To Practice?

Students are required to have a clean, white uniform, or gi, with an ISKF patch on the left breast area. (Students training at Gold's Gym are not
required to have a uniform unless they will be testing for rank.) Men are encouraged to wear a supporter and/or cup; women a sports bra or tee-
shirt underneath. This all the equipment you will need to practice. Additional items for the future include sparring gloves and a mouthpiece.
Supplemental equipment can include: Best Karate books, (volumes 1, 2 and 5 initially) videos of katas, a bag to carry your equipment and training
tape.

When Can I Start?

You can begin your training after you sign the waiver forms, pay your dues and have your uniform. Be on time or, even early, to class. It is
disrespectful to come late to class. If you are late, you must bow and be recognized before being allowed to enter. Do not embarrass yourself
further by disrupting the class in progress. Once you begin the class, you are expected to give your best effort. If you need to leave early, you must
let your instructor know in advance.

Karate Manners and Dojo Etiquette

“Karate begins and ends with courtesy”
Master Gichin Funakoshi

Shotokan is a traditional Japanese martial art and, as such, the guidelines of behavior and etiquette in the dojo are the same as if you were
training in Japan. You are required to show the proper respect to the shomen, sensei and the sempai (senior students) at all times. In addition, you
must show respect to the training facility by bowing when you enter and when you leave.

Bowing

Stand straight with your hands at your sides, heels together and toes at an angle of 45 degrees. Bend slowly forward until fingers touch the knees
and then stand up again. Do not slap your thighs or curl your neck.

When performing a seated bow, begin in the same position as the standing bow, and then put your left knee on the ground. Continue with the right
knee and sit on your heels with your back straight and looking toward the front. When instructed to bow, or rei, place both hands on the floor in front
of you and bend at the hips until your head touches your hands. Do not rise until the person you are bowing to rises. When told to stand, raise your
body vertically and place your right foot on the ground. Stand and bring both feet to musubi dachi. Do not place your hands on the floor in order to
get up or to kneel. Do not be concerned about making a mistake; you will be instructed in the proper procedure by another student on your first day.

Standing

It is impolite to stand with your arms crossed or hands on your hips. Stand in a natural position, relaxed with your weight on both feet. Hands can
be in front, behind or to the sides of your body.

Sitting

There are only two ways to sit in the dojo: formally sitting on your heels in zazen or seiza position and cross-legged. If you have a medical reason
where you cannot sit in these positions, you may be allowed to stand or kneel. Ask for permission beforehand.

Sensei/sempai/kohai

The sensei is the teacher or instructor. It loosely translates as, “the person who was there before the student.” Sempai is a senior student or
anyone who is a higher rank than you. They are kohai, or junior student, to the sensei. Children are to treat all adults as sempai; even if the child is
a higher rank. This is not to imply the adult can disrespect the ranking child; in this case, mutual respect is warranted. When addressed by a
senior student, the junior student should say, osu, and listen attentively. It is disrespectful to look around, show “attitude,” or ignore what they say.
They are there to help you; listen and learn.

Responsibilities/ Duties

It is your dojo regardless if it is in a gymnasium or a basketball court. When you enter the training area, you are to bow and say, osu, loudly to let
people know you are there. The same is true when you leave. If you are late, you will need to bow in formally, say osu, and wait quietly to be invited
into class.

Since we practice in facilities that have more than one use, we must remember we are guests in their space and should behave accordingly. We  
should leave the space better than we found it. We can usually get a broom to sweep the floor or at least we can pick up any larger pieces of trash
we see. Locker rooms must be kept neat. Do not leave your clothes on the floor or on the benches. This is not your room and sensei is not your
mother.

Your gi, or uniform should always be clean. Ideally, you should have at least two so you can wear one while the other is washed. Try not to wear
your uniform for more than two practices without washing; your fellow karate-ka will appreciate it. Your belt, or obi, is to be tied properly. A senior
student will instruct you on tying the knot on your first day. Learn it! Sensei does not tie belts for you. Your belt should never touch the floor unless
you are wearing it. Any belts seen on the floor will be confiscated and not returned until you show that you deserve to wear it again.
Your uniform should have an ISKF patch over the left breast and no other patches or decorations. If you have experience in another style, as a
courtesy, you may wear your old uniform and rank until your first test. Thereafter, you can only wear a plain white uniform and rank you were
awarded at your test.

Women are to wear a white tee-shirt or sports bra under their uniform; many people wear compression shorts, supporters, etc. under their
uniforms. This is allowed as long as it is not visible.  

Fingernails and toenails need to be trimmed to avoid injuries to yourself and others. Jewelry, watches and rings cannot be worn during training. If
you have a piercing that is permanent or not easily removed, you must wear athletic tape over it at every practice or event. No food, gum, smoking
or alcohol is allowed in the dojo. Profanity is prohibited. Running and rough play is not allowed unless it is part of the training. Sensei is the only
person that can make that distinction.

It is the responsibility of all current students to introduce themselves to the new students and to make them feel welcome. Offer to help them with
their practice, answer their questions; in other words, treat them as you want to be treated. In this dojo, we are all one club, one family.

When moving around the floor, remember to pass behind other people when changing positions. If you have to move farther than two steps, you
must run to your destination. If you are summoned by a senior student or the instructor, you say, osu, and run to them. When you are assigned to
work with a senior student in practice, you run to them.  

It is your duty to inform the sensei if you are injured, ill, or not attending class for an extended period. Let your instructor know before class if you
need to leave early. Try not to disrupt the class as you leave. A leave of absence needs to be discussed with the instructor. Speak to the instructor if
you decide to stop training altogether. It is the polite way to leave and will enable you to return later on good terms.

Class Procedures and Protocol

When the class begins, students will line up facing shomen (the front of the dojo), in musubi dachi, according to rank, with the higher ranking
member to your left. Sensei will take a position in front of the class and sit in zazen (formal seated position). The ranking student will announce:

  • Seiza!  This is the command to sit in the formal seated position. Students will kneel, in order of rank, until everyone is seated. You will be
    instructed about these procedures on your first day by a senior student.
  • Mokuso! This is a brief period of meditation, self reflection or personal preparation. You close your eyes, breathe in slowly through your
    nose and then exhale slowly through your mouth. Remain still and quiet.
  • Mokuso Yame! Open your eyes and remain quietly seated.
  • Shomen ni Rei! Bow to the front of the dojo. Please note: This has no religious significance. It is only showing respect to Masters
    Funakoshi, Nakayama and Okazaki.
  • Sensei ni Rei! Bow to sensei. You should say, osu!, and maintain your bow until sensei finishes bowing. When you rise, you must wait for
    the person to your left to stand before you stand. Once everyone is standing, you may move to your position on the floor.

Once everyone is in position, a sempai will lead the class in warm up. Proper respect and attitude will be expected at all times. You are in class to
train. Interruptions and misbehaving will not be tolerated! It is not only rude and disrespectful, it is also dangerous. Safety is our first concern. If you
need to leave the floor for any reason, you must have sensei’s permission. There are no breaks for drinks or to use the restroom; do these things
before class starts. If it is excessively hot or the training has been very difficult, sensei may allow a break. If so, be quick and return promptly.

Your karate training will consist mainly of three parts, kihon, kata and kumite. Never ask sensei, “What are we going to do today?” It is considered
rude and impolite to ask. You are there to do whatever you are asked to do. Ideally, we will work on all aspects of karate in each class; however,
this may not always be the case. Be prepared for anything. Have your gloves and mouth guard with you for each class. Training includes:

  • Kihon - These are the basics of karate. It includes all blocks, strikes, punches, kicks and stances.
  • Kata - The kata, or forms, are a series of blocks, strikes, kicks, punches and stances combined to simulate an attack by several
    opponents. Every kata begins with a block to foster the idea of karate as a method of self-defense, not attack.
  • Kumite - This is sparring or fighting. Kumite is geared specifically to each level of training and gets progressively more intense as the
    student increases in rank.

At the end of class, we often have cool-down stretches, partner stretches or, in the case of the children’s classes, “animal exercises.” These last
exercises are done when we have extra time and are designed to build muscle and stamina in the children, most of whom are too young to be
lifting weights. They consist of twelve sets of movements, most named after the animal they resemble. The group includes: monkey, bear, seal,
cricket, duck walk, duck hop, frog, spider, bunny, crab, elephant, and military crawl. We also do suicide sprints, jogging, and other cardiovascular
exercises in the dojo.

The command to line up signals the end of the class. Students are to run and line up in musubi dachi. You will line up in the same order as you
did at the beginning of class. The ranking sempai will announce:

  • Seiza! Sit in the same manner as in the beginning of class.
  • Mokuso! Close your eyes and clear your mind.
  • Mokuso Yame! Open your eyes.
  • Dojo Kun! This is the reciting of the dojo rules aloud. Senior students are expected to know it both in English and Japanese. NOTE:  The
    Dojo Kun will be listed later in these rules.
  • Shomen ni Rei! Bow to the front.
  • Sensei ni Rei! Bow to sensei and say, Domo arigato gozaimashita, or Thank you very much. Sensei will stand and signal the sempai to
    stand. You will wait to stand until the person on your left stands. Everyone then bows again, standing, and leaves quickly and quietly.

Belts/Rank/Testing

Originally, there were no belts in karate except those used to hold up your pants. Master Jigoro Kano, the founder of judo, instituted a belt ranking
system to differentiate between beginner and senior students. The first belts were only white (beginner) and black (senior). Soon, it became
necessary to have an intermediate level (brown). Eventually, another color, (green) was added between white and brown. This has now evolved
into the rainbow of colors we have currently. In Shotokan, the belts and ranks are:

White………………….....10th kyu
White w/ black stripe…..9th kyu
Yellow…………………...8th kyu
Orange………………… .7th kyu
Green…………………. ..6th kyu
Purple…………………. .5th - 4th kyu
Brown…………………. .3rd - 1st kyu
Black………………… …1st - 10th dan

White through orange are considered beginner ranks, green and purple are intermediate, and brown and black are advanced ranks. It will take, at
minimum, four years training before you can apply to test for dan rank or black belt.

Testing is an opportunity for students to advance in rank. Tests are scheduled three times per year, usually in January, May and September.
Members must have accrued, at least, 26 classes before being allowed to test. This is an average of about two classes per week, which is the
minimum number of classes someone can train. Students testing must pay an exam fee and, if membership has expired, a one year membership
fee to the ISKF. There is no guarantee that you will pass. Remember, it is not about belts; it is about perfecting your character.

Tournaments/Camps/Clinics

The ISKF hosts several tournaments per year. Regional tournaments are held in each of the ISKF districts around the country. Regional winners
and selected invitees are then eligible for the ISKF Nationals which are hosted by a different region each year. The United States Shotokan team is
selected from the winners and exceptional participants nominated by the tournament judges. Each tournament has events for children, collegiate,
senior and advanced ranks. Tournaments at Master and Fall camps have events for adults of all ranks. It is strongly encouraged that each
member of the dojo participates in ISKF tournaments whenever possible.

Master Camp and Fall Camp are held in June and September, respectively. Master Camp welcomes Shotokan participants from around the world
to train for up to a week with some of the best instructors from around the world. Fall Camp is held during a weekend and features training by the
most elite regional instructors available.

Many times during the year, senior instructors are invited to give special trainings at local clubs. We offer special training events prior to our kyu
exams and put this information on our website so that neighboring clubs can attend as well. There is usually a small fee required to compensate
the instructor and their expenses. Again, you are encouraged to attend.

Niju Kun
  1. Dojo nomino karate to omou na.
    Karate is not only for dojo training.
  1. Karate-do wa rei ni hajimari, rei ni owaru koto wo wasuruna.
    Karate begins and ends with a bow.
  1. Karate ni sente nashi.
    There is no first attack in karate.
  1. Karate wa gi no tasuke.
    One who practices karate follows the ways of justice.
  1. Mazu jiko wo shire, shikoshite tao wo shire.
    First know your self; then know others.
  1. Gijutsu yori shinjutsu.
    Kime first; technique second.
  1. Kokoro wa hanatan koto wo yosu.
    Release your mind.
  1. Wazawai wa getai ni shozu.
    Misfortune is a result of laziness.
  1. Karate no shugyo wa issho de aru.
    It takes a lifetime to learn karate.
  1. Arai-yuru mono wo karate-ka seyo, soko ni myo-mi ari.
    Make karate a part of everyday life.
  1. Karate wa yu no goto shi taezu natsudo wo ataezareba moto no mizu ni kaeru.
    Karate is like hot water. If you do not heat it, it grows cold.
  1. Katsu kangae wa motsu na makenu kangae wa hitsuyo.
    Do not focus on winning; focus on not losing.
  1. Tekki ni yotte tenka seyo.
    Victory depends on telling vulnerable points from non-vulnerable ones.
  1. Tattakai wa kyo-jitsu no soju ikan ni ari.
    Move according to your opponent.
  1. Hito no te ashi wo ken to omoe.
    Consider your hands and feet as sharp swords.
  1. Danshi mon wo izureba hyakuman no tekki ari.
    When you leave home, imagine millions of opponents are waiting to attack you.
  1. Kamae wa shoshinsha ni ato wa shizentai.
    Ready position for beginners; natural position for advanced.
  1. Kata wa tadashiku jissen wa betsu mono.
    Kata is one thing; a real fight is another.
  1. Chikara no kyojaku, karada no shinshuku, waza no kankyu wo wasaruna.
    Remember: strength and weakness of power; expansion and contraction of body; slowness and speed of techniques.
  1. Tsune ni shinen kufu seyo
    Always create and devise.
